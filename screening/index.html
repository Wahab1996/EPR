FM Screening Guidelines – Saudi Arabia

Breast Cancer

Women aged 40–74: Mammography every 1–2 years. Start earlier if family history of breast or ovarian cancer. Stop at life expectancy <10 years.

Sources — USPSTF 2024, MOH

Cervical Cancer

Women aged 21–29: Pap smear every 3 years. Women 30–65: Pap + HPV cotesting every 5 years preferred. Stop after 65 if adequate prior screening and low risk.

Source — USPSTF 2023

Colorectal Cancer

Adults aged 45–75: Annual FIT, or colonoscopy every 10 years. Continue to 85 in select high-risk individuals.

Source — USPSTF 2021

Diabetes Mellitus (Type 2)

Adults ≥35 years or ≥18 if overweight/obese with risk factors (family history, PCOS, GDM). Repeat every 3 years if normal.

Sources — USPSTF 2021, MOH

Hypertension

Adults ≥18 years: Check BP at least every 1–2 years if normal, annually if ≥120/80 mmHg.

Sources — USPSTF, ACC/AHA 2017

Dyslipidemia

Men ≥35 and women ≥45: Lipid panel every 5 years. Screen earlier if risk factors (DM, smoking, HTN, family history).

Source — USPSTF

Obesity

All adults & children ≥6 years: Screen for obesity using BMI & waist circumference. Counsel if BMI ≥25 kg/m².

Sources — USPSTF, WHO

Hepatitis B & C

HBV: Screen if high-risk (dialysis, household/sexual contact).
HCV: One-time screen for adults 18–79, earlier if IV drug use or high-risk.

Sources — CDC, USPSTF 2020

Tuberculosis (TB)

Screen high-risk (contacts, healthcare workers, migrants from high TB areas) using TST or IGRA.

Sources — WHO, MOH

Depression & Anxiety

All adults & adolescents ≥12 years: Screen annually using PHQ-9 & GAD-7. Pay attention in postpartum women.

Sources — USPSTF, MOH

Smoking & Substance Use

Ask all ≥12 years about tobacco (cigarettes, shisha) & substance use. Counsel and offer cessation aids.

Sources — USPSTF, WHO

Premarital Screening

Mandatory for couples before marriage: includes hemoglobinopathies (sickle cell, thalassemia), hepatitis B/C, HIV.

Source — MOH Saudi Premarital Program

Osteoporosis

Women ≥65: DEXA scan. Younger postmenopausal women if fracture risk ≥9.3%. Men if risk factors.

Source — USPSTF 2018
